Textile Art

I first learned how to crochet when I was nine years old, and I learned how to sew on a sewing machine shortly afterwards. I then began to sell the items that I made to family and friends while adding embroidery and knitting to my repertoire. I have had many hours of practice with these skills, so I think that I can call myself an expert! Below is a table of textile art FAQ.

Question Answer
Which form of textile art is easiest to learn? I would say that sewing on a machine is the easiest--the machine does most of the work, and you usually just have to guide it in a straight line. Also, following sewing patterns is fairly simple.
How do you learn how to make a specific item? There are patterns available online for all of these activities--if I have something that I want to make, whether it's a sewing, knitting, or crocheting project, I will look online for free patterns.
Is it useful to know these skills? Although making things by hand is often tedious and time-consuming, a textile art project is my go-to gift for the people I love, and the time and effort I put into the project makes the gift extra special. These skills have also been useful for altering clothing, making cheaper versions of expensive items, and general relaxation.
Is textile art actually an art? Absolutely! I think that textile art is a hugely underrated art form that is often overlooked because of its history as a task (or chore, even) that only women do. There have actually been an increasing number of textile art installments and exhibits in major museums in the last few decades.
What sort of materials do you need to make textile art? For crocheting and knitting, you just need yarn and a hook or needle. For sewing and embroidery, you need fabric and thread, and a machine if you would like (but not required). There are a few extra tools that you can purchase, like embroidery hoops, gauge rulers, stitch markers, and fabric chalk, but these are the basics.
Do these skills require your full focus? Following complicated patterns usually requires my full focus, but I will usually do these activities while watching a show or talking on the phone. It took a few months of practice to be able to multi-task this way, but it has now been many years.
What are some good beginner projects? For crocheting and knitting, some good starter projects are hats or scarves, since these involve going back and forth in rows or working in a consistent circle. For sewing, you could begin with a simple pouch or tote bag.
How did you learn these skills? I was initially taught by my aunt, who is a middle school art teacher, but I expanded my skills by watching online tutorials and trying to follow patterns for things that I wanted to make.
Can you make things without following a pattern? Yes! After developing a solid foundation (of textile art primitives, if you will), I started to make things without following a pattern. I actually found that there is some engineering involved in this process--I have an idea of the final product that I want to make, and then I decompose the product into parts that can be assembled, and then I think about how I would make each part. This is often a process of trial and error.
Is there a connection between textile art and computer science? Surprisingly yes! The Jacquard loom was the first loom that could knit complicated patterns (with individual thread control), and it is actually considered a predecessor to the modern computer because it demonstrated the concept of programmable machines using punch cards. The way the loom used stored instructions to automate a task directly inspired Charles Babbage's Analytical Machine, and Ada Lovelace refers to it in her work.
